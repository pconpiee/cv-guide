Section 1

Structure & Section Order

How you arrange your CV is a strategic decision. Recruiters scan in an F-pattern ‚Äî top-left first, then diagonally. Put your most relevant, impressive content in the first third of the page.

üîç
Senior Recruiter

I scan your CV in under 10 seconds before deciding whether to keep reading. If I can't find your current title, company, and one impressive outcome in that first glance ‚Äî you're in the reject pile. Put the most relevant signal at the very top.

üìã
Head of Talent

Section order signals what you value. Experienced hires should lead with a summary and work history ‚Äî not education. Recent grads can flip that. And never start with an "Objective" statement; it's about what you want, not what you offer.

üëî
Hiring Manager

I'm looking for trajectory. I want to see that your career is moving in a clear direction and that each role built on the last. Your structure should tell a story of growth, not just list jobs. Show me you're ready for this next step.

1
Header ‚Äî Name, Title, Contact
Full name (large), your professional title (match the job posting), email, phone, LinkedIn URL, city/country. No photo, no age, no marital status. Keep it clean and scannable.
‚ö†Ô∏è Use a professional email. yourname@gmail.com, not coolkid99@hotmail.com
2
Professional Summary (2‚Äì4 lines)
This is your elevator pitch. One sentence on who you are, one on your key strength, one on what you're seeking. Tailor this for every single job application. This is the most-read part of your CV.
üí° Never write "I am a hardworking professional seeking..." ‚Äî use third-person positioning: "Product leader with 7 years..."
3
Work Experience (reverse chronological)
Most recent role first. For each role: company, title, dates, location, then 3‚Äì6 bullet points. Focus on impact, not duties. Use strong action verbs. Include at least one metric per role.
üí° Spend 70% of your CV real estate on your most recent 2 roles
4
Skills
Keep it concise and relevant. Split into hard skills (tools, languages, platforms) and soft competencies. Don't rate yourself with stars or bars ‚Äî they're meaningless and waste space.
‚ö†Ô∏è Only include skills you can speak to confidently in an interview
5

Degree, institution, graduation year, notable achievements. If you graduated more than 5 years ago, move this after Work Experience. If you're a recent grad, keep it near the top.
üí° Include GPA only if it's strong (3.5+) and you're early-career
6
Additional (Projects, Certifications, Languages)
Side projects, freelance work, relevant certifications, languages spoken. Only include if directly relevant to the role. This section can be powerful for career changers or recent grads.
üí° A well-described side project can outweigh 2 years of irrelevant experience
‚úì Do

1‚Äì2 pages maximum

1 page for <10 years experience. 2 pages for senior roles. Academic CVs are the exception. Every line must earn its place.

‚úì Do

Consistent formatting throughout

Same font, same date format (Jan 2022 ‚Äì Mar 2024), same bullet style. Inconsistency signals carelessness.

‚úó Don't

Everyone knows. It just wastes a line. Only include references when specifically asked.

‚úó Don't

US, UK, Canada: never. Some European countries expect it. Always check the local norm for the market you're applying in.

‚ö† Watch out

Section 2

Beating the ATS Filter

Most large companies use Applicant Tracking Systems. Your CV is scanned by software before a human ever reads it. Fail the ATS, and you're invisible ‚Äî even if you're the perfect candidate.

How ATS works

An ATS parses your CV, extracts information, and scores it against the job description. It looks for keyword matches, section headers it recognizes, and structured data it can read. Fancy tables, columns, icons, and graphics often break ATS parsing entirely ‚Äî your information disappears.

üîç
Senior Recruiter

I've seen brilliant candidates eliminated because their CV was built in Canva with two-column layouts and icons. Beautiful to look at ‚Äî invisible to our ATS. Simple, clean, single-column beats fancy every time for online applications.

üìã
Head of Talent

The fix is simple: copy the exact language from the job posting. If they say "cross-functional stakeholder management," don't write "worked with different teams." Mirror their vocabulary. The ATS is literally counting keyword matches.

üëî
Hiring Manager

I write the job description. When I later read CVs, I'm pattern-matching to the language I used. It's not gaming the system ‚Äî it's communication. Show me you understand what we actually need by reflecting it back.

‚úì ATS-Safe

Single-column layout

Multi-column designs confuse ATS parsers. Keep everything in one linear flow ‚Äî name, then summary, then experience, etc.

‚úì ATS-Safe

Standard section headers

Use recognized headers: "Work Experience" not "Where I've Been." "Education" not "My Journey." ATS looks for known labels.

‚úì ATS-Safe

Mirror the job posting's language

Pull key phrases directly from the job description. Weave them naturally into your bullets and summary ‚Äî don't just keyword-stuff at the bottom.

‚úó ATS-Killer

Tables, text boxes, columns

ATS parsers often can't read content inside tables or text boxes. That skills table you think looks clean? It may be completely invisible.

‚úó ATS-Killer

Graphics, icons, progress bars

Skill bars showing "Excel: ‚ñà‚ñà‚ñà‚ñà‚ñë‚ñë" communicate nothing meaningful to a human and nothing at all to an ATS. Remove them.

‚ö† Grey area

Abbreviations without spelling out

Write "Search Engine Optimization (SEO)" the first time ‚Äî ATS may only know one version. Same for role titles, tools, and frameworks.

Section 3

Writing Powerful Bullets

A CV bullet is not a job description. It's a proof point. Every bullet must answer: what did you do, how did you do it, and why does it matter?

The "So What?" Test

Before you submit any bullet, apply this filter. If you can't answer each question, the bullet isn't done yet.

  • 1
    What exactly did you do? (action + subject)
  • 2
    What was the size/scale of it? (team size, budget, user count, market size)
  • 3
    What was the result? (revenue, time saved, % improvement, award, new customers)
  • 4
    Who else benefited? (team, company, customers, industry)
  • 5
    Why does this matter to this job? (connect it to their needs)
üîç
Senior Recruiter

The single biggest red flag I see? "Responsible for..." ‚Äî it tells me what you were supposed to do, not what you actually accomplished. I want to see what happened because you showed up. Also: never write "I" in a CV bullet.

üìã
Head of Talent

Each bullet should tie to a specific responsibility in the job posting. I stack-rank candidates by how many of our stated requirements they've demonstrably done before. If your bullets don't match our language, you're invisible ‚Äî even if you've done the work.

üëî
Hiring Manager

I'm looking for proof of judgment, not just activity. Anyone can write "led meetings." Show me you made decisions, solved hard problems, and influenced outcomes. The best bullets make me think: "This person has been in the trenches."

Passive vs. Active
‚ùå Weak
Responsible for managing the social media accounts for the company.
Uses "responsible for" ‚Äî says what you were supposed to do, not what you did. No metric, no outcome, no scale.
‚úì Strong
Led team of 3 to execute a referral program across 4 social platforms, generating $50K in new revenue and 120 new customers in Q3.
Strong verb. Team size. Platform count. Two metrics. Time-bounded. Specific.
Generic vs. Specific
‚ùå Weak
Led user research on our products.
What kind of research? How many users? What happened as a result? This tells me almost nothing.
‚úì Strong
Synthesized qualitative research from 23 usability tests into 47 actionable tickets; worked with 4 engineers to confirm build meets 79 critical user specifications.
Method. Sample size. Output. Team size. Measurable acceptance criteria. This is a full story in one line.
Duty vs. Impact
‚ùå Weak
Attended and contributed to weekly strategy meetings with the C-suite.
"Attended" is not an achievement. "Contributed" is vague. What changed because you were there?
‚úì Strong
Presented weekly product metrics to CEO and 3 VPs; analysis directly shaped company's pivot to enterprise market, contributing to $1.2M ARR in 6 months.
Audience specified. Role clear. Causal link to business outcome. Metric with time frame.
Vague vs. Vivid
‚ùå Weak
Helped improve the onboarding process which was not working well.
What was the problem? What did you do? What improved? "Helped" undersells you and "not working well" is too vague.
‚úì Strong
Redesigned 6-step onboarding flow after analyzing 150 drop-off events; reduced time-to-activation from 14 days to 3, increasing 30-day retention by 22%.
Problem is clear. Scope is clear. Method is clear. Quantified before/after. Two distinct metrics.

Section 6

Finding Your Numbers

Most people think they have no metrics. They're wrong. Every role has numbers ‚Äî you just have to know where to look. Here's how to find them.

üîç
Senior Recruiter

CVs with at least 3 quantified bullets get significantly more callbacks in my experience. You don't need exact figures ‚Äî approximations and ranges are fine. "Reduced churn by ~15%" beats "improved customer retention" every single time.

üìã
Head of Talent

Ask your manager. Seriously. Before you leave any job, ask: "Can you share the business metrics for the projects I led?" Most managers will give you rough figures. If you can't get exact numbers, use publicly available data ‚Äî company revenue, published reports, product pages.

üëî
Hiring Manager

Even soft roles have numbers. How many stakeholders? How many sessions? What was the budget? Quantity is everywhere ‚Äî you just haven't been writing it down. Start doing so weekly, while it's fresh. A number doesn't have to be revenue to matter.

üí∞ Business Impact

  • Revenue generated or influenced
  • Cost savings achieved
  • ARR / MRR growth
  • Deals closed or supported
  • Budget managed ($X)
  • Fundraise or grant secured

üìà Performance %

  • % improvement in metric
  • Growth vs. prior period
  • Efficiency gains (time, cost)
  • Error reduction rate
  • Churn / retention change
  • NPS or CSAT score shift

üë• People & Scale

  • Team size managed
  • Number of stakeholders
  • Users / customers affected
  • Countries or regions
  • Candidates hired / onboarded
  • Events attended / organized

‚è± Time & Volume

  • Time saved (hours/week)
  • Time to deliver (from X to Y days)
  • Number of projects simultaneously
  • Tickets / issues resolved
  • Reports / documents produced
  • Training hours delivered
No exact number? Use a range or qualifier. "Reduced processing time by ~40%," "Managed a team of 8‚Äì12 contractors," "Generated ‚Ç¨500K‚Äì‚Ç¨700K in pipeline." Approximate honesty beats no number at all.
Section 7

Common Pitfalls & How to Fix Them

These are the mistakes that appear in the majority of CVs. Each one reduces your chances. Each one is fixable in under 5 minutes.

üö©

"Responsible for..." bullets

This phrasing describes your job description, not your achievements. It tells the recruiter what you were supposed to do ‚Äî not what you actually accomplished.

Replace every "Responsible for X" with a verb + outcome. "Responsible for client onboarding" ‚Üí "Onboarded 40 enterprise clients across 3 verticals, achieving 94% 90-day retention."
üö©

One generic CV for all applications

Recruiters and ATS systems can tell when a CV was written for a different job. The language doesn't match the posting. The emphasis is wrong. The summary describes someone else's career.

Build a master CV with all your bullets, then create a tailored version for every application ‚Äî reorder bullets, update the summary, mirror the job posting's language.
üö©

No professional summary

Jumping straight to work history means you're making the recruiter figure out who you are and what you're looking for. Don't make them guess.

Add a 2‚Äì3 line summary at the top: [Role title] with [X] years of experience in [domain], known for [key strength], currently seeking [type of role].
üö©

Unexplained employment gaps

Gaps aren't disqualifying ‚Äî but unexplained gaps trigger concern. Recruiters' imaginations will fill the gap with something worse than the truth.

Address gaps briefly and honestly: "Career break (parental leave)," "Freelance consulting (2022‚Äì2023)," "Personal health ‚Äî fully resolved." One line is enough. Own it.
üö©

Soft skills without evidence

"Excellent communicator," "strong team player," "results-oriented." These phrases appear in 90% of CVs and say nothing. They're claims without proof.

Either remove soft skill claims entirely, or prove them with a bullet: instead of "strong leader," write "Led team of 7 through a 6-month re-org with 0 voluntary attrition."
üö©

Outdated or irrelevant experience

The part-time job from 12 years ago is not helping you. Roles older than 10‚Äì15 years should either be compressed into a single line or removed entirely.

For older roles: compress to one line ‚Äî "Junior Analyst, ABC Corp (2008‚Äì2011)." Only expand if it contains a directly relevant achievement you can't prove elsewhere.
üö©

Obvious AI writing ("Spearheaded synergistic initiatives")

Recruiters are now trained to spot AI-generated CVs. Overused phrases, suspiciously perfect grammar, and a generic tone signal that you didn't write your own story ‚Äî and can't be bothered to.

Use AI as a first draft, then rewrite in your own voice. Make sure every bullet sounds like something you'd actually say. Add specific details only you would know.
üö©

Claiming skills you can't demonstrate

Listing a tool or skill you barely know is a liability. Hiring managers will ask you about every line in the interview. If you're asked to demonstrate it and can't, the offer disappears.

Only list skills you can speak confidently about for 5+ minutes. If you're learning something, list it as "Currently developing: [skill]" ‚Äî this shows honesty and growth mindset.
Section 8

Special Situations

Not every career path is linear. Here's how to handle the situations most guides ignore.

Handling Employment Gaps

The principle: Brief, honest, forward-facing. Don't hide it ‚Äî that signals shame. Don't over-explain it ‚Äî that signals defensiveness. One line is usually enough.

  • Parental leave: "Career break ‚Äî parental leave (Jan 2023‚ÄìAug 2023)"
  • Health: "Personal medical leave ‚Äî fully resolved (2022)" ‚Äî no need for more detail
  • Layoff: Simply show the end date honestly. "Downsizing" is widely understood and not a disqualifier
  • Travel / Sabbatical: "Career break ‚Äî independent travel and personal development" is completely acceptable in most industries
  • Freelance during the gap: List it as experience ‚Äî "Freelance [role], 2022‚Äì2023" with bullets showing what you did

Key insight from Head of Talent: Gaps up to 12 months are now so common post-COVID that most experienced recruiters don't flag them. What matters is what you've done most recently.

Changing Career or Industry

The principle: Lead with transferable skills and reframe past experience through the lens of the new industry.

  • Summary is critical: Your summary must make your pivot explicit ‚Äî "Former educator transitioning into UX research, bringing 8 years of behavioral observation and curriculum design"
  • Reframe bullets: Highlight the aspects of your old role that overlap with the new one. A teacher "managed classrooms of 30, facilitated complex concepts, and analyzed performance data" ‚Äî that's project management, communication, and analytics
  • Fill gaps with side projects: Build something in the new field. Write about it. Certify. One real project beats five listed courses
  • Don't apologize for the change: Own the narrative ‚Äî "After 7 years in X, I've developed [transferable skill] and now bring that foundation to [new field]"

Recent Graduate with Limited Experience

The principle: Depth over breadth. One well-described project beats ten vague internships.

  • Lead with Education if you graduated within 2 years and your degree is relevant
  • Projects are experience. A thesis, capstone, hackathon, or open-source contribution counts. Describe it like a real job: what was the goal, what did you do, what was the result?
  • Internships: Treat them like full roles ‚Äî give them bullet points with metrics, not just title + company + "assisted the team"
  • Volunteering and leadership: Club president, society founder, event organizer ‚Äî these are leadership experiences. Write them properly
  • Don't pad: A 1-page focused CV from a grad beats a 2-page one filled with high school achievements and irrelevant part-time work

Hiring Manager insight: I'm evaluating your potential and learning speed, not your resume length. Show me one thing you built, broke, or improved ‚Äî and tell me what you learned.

Military ‚Üí Civilian Transition

The principle: Translate military language into civilian business value. Your experience is extensive ‚Äî the challenge is making it legible to a civilian recruiter who may have no military context.

  • Translate rank and titles: "O-4 / Major" ‚Üí "Senior Manager" or "Department Head." Explain what the rank meant in terms of responsibility, not hierarchy
  • Translate missions into outcomes: "Led 23-person unit in GPS-degraded environments" ‚Üí "Managed a 23-person cross-functional team to execute high-stakes operations under zero-visibility conditions with 100% mission success rate"
  • Budget and resources are universal: "Responsible for $4.2M in assets and equipment" translates perfectly. Use it
  • Leadership scale is your advantage: Most 30-year-old civilians have never led a team of 20. Lean into this heavily
  • Avoid jargon: CONOPS, MOS, SITREP, TTP ‚Äî spell everything out the first time, then consider if the acronym adds anything at all
  • Connect to the company mission: Many veterans undersell themselves by not connecting their "why" to the civilian company's mission. Do this explicitly in your summary

Showing Career Growth & Trajectory

The principle: Hiring managers want to hire someone on an upward trajectory. Your CV should visibly show that each role was bigger, broader, or more impactful than the last.

  • Show progression at the same company: List promotions as separate roles: "Senior Engineer (2022‚Äìpresent)" above "Engineer (2020‚Äì2022)" ‚Äî same company, two entries
  • Make scope expansion explicit: Use your bullets to show team sizes growing, budgets increasing, markets expanding over time
  • Connect roles with intent: Your summary should make the through-line clear: "7-year career arc from analyst to department head, consistently expanding scope across [domain]"
  • Don't hide lateral moves: Frame them as breadth-building ‚Äî "Moved laterally from X to Y to build cross-functional expertise in Z, which enabled..."

Head of Talent insight: I look at the trajectory, not just the last role. If I see genuine progression ‚Äî more complexity, more ownership, more scope ‚Äî I'm already sold before I read a single bullet.
